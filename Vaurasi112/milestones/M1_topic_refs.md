# Milestone 1 — Topic + Initial References

## 1. Topic
Procrastination in university students during the academic term; competing explanations based on emotion regulation failure and temporal decision-making

## 2. Rationale
Procrastination is a common behaviour among university students and is consistently associated with lower academic performance, increased stress, and reduced well-being. Despite extensive research, there is no single agreed explanation for why procrastination occurs. Two dominant perspectives suggest different mechanisms: one argues that procrastination is primarily driven by difficulties in regulating negative emotions associated with tasks, while another proposes that procrastination reflects rational decision-making processes involving the devaluation of future rewards. Understanding which explanation better accounts for behaviour has implications for designing effective academic interventions.

## 3. Scope
- Population: the focus group would be the university students aged (18>30)
- Context:  Focuses on how academic task such as studying, assignments and exam preparations lead to procrastination                
- Included: Non-clinical procrastination behaviour in higher education settings
- Excluded: Clinical populations (e.g., ADHD diagnoses), workplace procrastination, adolescents under 18, and non-academic tasks  

## 4. Competing Explanations


### Theory A: Emotion Regulation Failure
This explanation proposes that procrastination occurs when individuals avoid tasks that trigger negative emotional states such as anxiety, boredom, or frustration. Delaying the task provides short-term emotional relief, which reinforces avoidance behaviour over time. Procrastination is therefore conceptualised as a maladaptive coping strategy for managing immediate emotional discomfort.

### Theory B: Temporal Decision-Making
This explanation argues that procrastination results from how individuals value immediate versus delayed rewards. Individuals tend to overvalue immediate comfort and undervalue future outcomes 

## 5. Research Questions
1. To what extent do emotional states predict procrastination behaviour compared to cognitive decision-making processes?
2. Under what conditions does temporal discounting better explain procrastination in students?
3. Are interventions targeting emotion regulation more effective than time-management strategies in reducing procrastination?
4. How does academic stress influence the strength of each explanatory model?
5. Which explanation better predicts procrastination under high-pressure deadlines?
