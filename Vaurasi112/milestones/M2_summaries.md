## Article 1 (Emotion regulation theory)

Procrastination and emotion regulation research

### Full citation
Sirois, F. M., & Pychyl, T. A. (2013)
Procrastination and the priority of short-term mood regulation
Social and Personality Psychology Compass
https://doi.org/10.1111/spc3.12011

### Introduction
This paper examines procrastination as a self-regulation failure This paper looks at procrastination as a failure to manage oneself, mainly caused by feelings. The authors say that people put off tasks to avoid negative emotions like anxiety or frustration that come with completing those tasks. Driven primarily by emotional processes. The authors argue that individuals delay tasks to avoid negative emotional states such as anxiety or frustration associated with task completion.

### Methods
This paper reviews existing research on procrastination and emotion. It brings together previous experimental and correlational studies to provide a clearer understanding of these topics.

### Results
The review shows that procrastination is closely connected to trying to feel better in the short term. People tend to put off tasks when they think they will feel bad.

### Discussion
Sirois and Pychyl (2013) say that procrastination, or putting things off, happens because people want to feel better right now. They don’t avoid tasks because they can't do them, but because the tasks make them feel bad or anxious. When people skip these tasks, they feel good for a little bit. But later, they may feel more stressed or guilty when deadlines come up. This creates a conflict between wanting to feel good now and dealing with problems later. The authors also note that their study is based on earlier research and not new experiments, which makes it harder to say exactly why people procrastinate. They suggest that more studies are needed to understand how people deal with these feelings over time.

### Cross-exam
The authors argue that procrastination mainly happens because people want to avoid negative feelings. This idea would be proven wrong if we found strong evidence that shows people still procrastinate when they feel neutral or good, or when we remove emotional issues in experiments. For example, if people delay tasks even when they are feeling positive, or if methods that reduce negative feelings do not help decrease procrastination, it would question the idea that mood regulation is the main cause. Also, if more powerful reasons, like misunderstandings about deadlines or task requirements, explain procrastination better than emotions in different studies, the mood-regulation concept would be less valid.

A key issue is that researchers sometimes confuse avoiding unpleasant tasks with emotional regulation. They might think that people delay tasks to manage their feelings, but it could actually be because tasks are too difficult, lack motivation, or are poorly designed. In this case, procrastination might instead be about avoiding tasks that offer low rewards or require too much effort. Another important factor is self-control or conscientiousness, which can affect how people feel about tasks and their tendency to delay them.

## Article 2 (decision-making / temporal discounting)

Temporal discounting and procrastination research



### Full citation
Steel, P. (2007)
The nature of procrastination: A meta-analytic and theoretical review
Psychological Bulletin
https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.133.1.65

### Introduction
Steel (2007) provides a thorough review of procrastination, aiming to explain its psychological causes. The paper argues that procrastination is a common issue where people fail to manage their time effectively. It uses Temporal Motivation Theory (TMT) to explain this. According to this theory, our motivation to finish a task depends on four factors: our belief in success, how appealing the task is, how we respond to delays, and the length of the delay itself. Steel's goal is to bring together many different studies on personality, motivation, and behavior to find the main reasons why people procrastinate and to understand why it happens in various situations.

### Methods
This study is a meta-analysis that combines results from many existing studies about procrastination. Steel looked at how procrastination is related to different psychological factors, including personality traits like conscientiousness and impulsivity, motivations such as the value of a task and the likelihood of success, and demographic factors like age and gender. The analysis included hundreds of effect sizes from various samples and research methods. Steel used statistical techniques to calculate average correlations, helping to determine the strength of different predictors across the studies.

### Results
The study found that procrastination mainly comes from low conscientiousness, high impulsiveness, low expectations for tasks, and not valuing tasks highly. It also identified task delays and sensitivity to delays as important factors, which fits with Temporal Motivation Theory. The research shows that procrastination is not significantly related to demographic factors like gender, but it is linked to personality traits and motivation. 

Overall, procrastination results from difficulties in self-regulation, influenced by both personality and motivation related to tasks. The results indicated that procrastination is most closely associated with low conscientiousness, followed by high impulsiveness, low task expectancy, and low task value. Task delay and sensitivity to delay were also important predictors, supporting the ideas of Temporal Motivation Theory. The study found that procrastination does not relate meaningfully to demographic variables like gender, but it does show a consistent link with personality and motivation. Overall, these findings suggest that procrastination is best understood as a failure of self-regulation driven by personality traits and motivation for tasks.


### Discussion
Steel concludes that procrastination is a strong and predictable behavior explained by Temporal Motivation Theory. This theory combines three factors: expectancy, value, and delay. The research shows that procrastination is not random; it follows consistent patterns related to personality, especially conscientiousness, and other motivational factors.
However, a key limitation is that much of the evidence comes from self-report measures, which can exaggerate the relationships due to similar responses. Also, because the data shows correlations, we cannot determine causation. It is unclear if low conscientiousness leads to procrastination or if both result from larger self-regulation issues. Despite these limitations, the analysis strongly supports the idea that procrastination is a stable behavior that can be measured and has recognizable psychological reasons behind it.


### Cross-exam
A major challenge to Steel’s (2007) conclusion would be if we found that procrastination couldn't be effectively reduced or predicted by changing key factors in Temporal Motivation Theory, like how much people expect to succeed, how valuable they find tasks, or how immediate the tasks are. For instance, if people still procrastinate even when tasks are made more rewarding, simpler, or more urgent, it would weaken the idea that procrastination is simply a result of rational decision-making. Additionally, if factors like emotional management or environmental issues consistently explain procrastination better than motivation-related factors, it would lessen the theory's credibility.

However, a big issue with interpreting Steel’s findings is that they rely heavily on self-reported data for both procrastination and its predictors, like conscientiousness and impulsiveness. This could lead to inflated correlations due to overlapping traits and biased reporting. Because of this, it’s hard to tell if the relationships we see are genuine causes or just reflections of how people view their own traits.
